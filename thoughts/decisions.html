A Path to Calm

Dishevelment is the norm for many, but it doesn't have to be if you clear the fog. One source of fog comes from decisions. Every decision – big or small – comes with its own sludge of thoughts to process, which is quite easy to get lost in. Too much of it causes wasted days, overwhelming moments and negative outcomes. No one wants these things… so reduce! Reduce events, information, or possessions to save energy and regain composure.

First, events. This category could include social obligations, new projects, travels. Every event comes with a bag of decisions; what to wear, when to leave, how to get there, what to eat, what to omit, how to begin, what tools, etc. I'm sure most have experienced the hour slump that pads meetings. Or difficulties starting a project. So, since they're difficult and energy expensive, I just choose a few carefully and toss the rest. My criteria is how much energy is burnt in exchange for benefits.1

Second, information. Endless information. The net is scary. Whether you're conscious of it or not, you have to decide every comment's worth. The dirty secret is most of it is useless. The less information you're processing, the less you have to filter and sort and decide upon. You can instead chew on information that is valuable by upgrading your source, like blogs.2 I limit information exposure by blocking YouTube sections or social sites, news sites, or anything else with uBlock Origin and my /etc/hosts file.

You have to decide every comment's worth.

And finally, possessions. Physical possessions. Virtual possessions. Mental possessions. It's a big category, and a little vague I'll admit. But you can see it as any sort of "thing" that attaches itself to you. Each item, physical or not, contributes to decision fatigue. So, I get rid of anything nonessential. Physical cleaning warrants no example.3 A virtual clean may include software reduction, or technology reduction. And a mental clean may be auditing what you read, how you perform tasks. The less you have attached to you and the less complicated things are, the less things in the way. Less you need to keep track of. Less decisions. It all results a clear focus and a calm mind.

If you begin to tackle just one of these categories you will feel better. The keyword is begin: it's a continual shed. It's not a one-and-done. Just taking 20 minutes every couple of days will grant you dividends. I hope these categories help you notice what may be out of control for you.
