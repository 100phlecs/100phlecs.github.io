#+title: A Path to Calm


Decisions, decisions... endless. The more choices you have to make,
the more foggy you will be. And tired. And that'll translate to wasted
days, overwhelming moments and frustrated feelings. If you want to
avoid that, then reduce! Reduce events, opinions, objects in your
life. This'll let you save cognition by reducing choices. With less to
focus on comes calmness from within. By reducing events, information,
and things, you will regain your composure.

[[file:../assets/decisions.png]]

First on the list is events. This category could include social
obligations, new projects, travels. Every event comes with a bag of
decisions; what to wear, when to leave, how to get there, what to eat,
what to omit, how to begin, what tools, etc. I'm sure most have
experienced the hour slump that pads meetings. Or difficulties
starting a project. So, since they're difficult, instead of letting
them stack up and take away my calm, I just choose a few carefully. I
choose on the criteria of how much energy is burnt in exchange for
benefits.[fn:1]

Second, information. Endless information.  The net is scary this way.
Whether you're conscious of it or not, you have to /decide/ every
comment's worth.  The dirty secret is most of it is useless.  The less
information you're processing, the less you have to filter and sort
and /decide/ upon.  You can instead hone in information that *is* valuable
by upgrading your source, like blogs.[fn:2] I limit information
exposure by blocking YouTube sections or social sites, news sites,
etc. with [[https://ublockorigin.com/][uBlock Origin]] and my [[https://github.com/StevenBlack/hosts][/etc/hosts file]].

And finally, possessions. Physical possessions. Virtual
possessions. Mental possessions. Each item, physical or not,
contributes to decision fatigue. It's a big category, and a little
vague I'll admit. But you can see it as any sort of "thing" that
attaches itself to you, or fades into the background (like
projects). Physical cleaning warrants no example.[fn:3] A virtual
clean may include software reduction, or usage reduction. And a mental
clean may be auditing what you read, how you perform tasks. The less
you have attached to you and the less complicated they are, then the
less steps needed to perform, the less you need to keep track of. Less
decisions. It all results a clear focus and a calm mind.

If you begin to tackle just one of these categories you will feel
better.  The keyword is begin: it's a continual shed. It's not a
one-and-done.  Just taking 20 minutes every couple of days will grant
you dividends.  I hope these categories help you notice what may be out
of control for you.
